Weathering the Storm: A Practical Guide to Preparing Your Home for Severe Weather

2026-06-18 11:06 UTC · 💡 How-To

With unpredictable weather patterns making headlines globally, from sudden downpours in Atlanta to widespread storm alerts, it’s clear that Mother Nature keeps us on our toes. While we can’t control the weather, we *can* control how prepared our homes and families are when a severe storm hits. Taking a few proactive steps can save you stress, damage, and even keep your loved ones safe. Here’s a practical how-to guide to get your home storm-ready.

Stay Informed and Ahead of the Curve

The first line of defense is always information. Sign up for local emergency alerts, download a reliable weather app, and tune into local news channels during severe weather events. Knowing what’s coming – whether it’s heavy rain, high winds, or potential tornadoes – gives you precious time to act. Don't wait until the sky turns dark to start thinking about preparation.

Secure Your Outdoors

Your yard can become a projectile hazard in high winds. Walk around your property and secure or bring indoors anything that could blow away. This includes patio furniture, trash cans, grills, and garden decorations. Trim any dead or weak tree branches that could fall and damage your home or power lines. Clear gutters and downspouts to ensure proper water drainage, preventing potential flooding around your foundation.

Prepare Your Indoors for Safety

Inside your home, close and lock all windows and doors. If you have storm shutters, deploy them. For homes without shutters, consider cutting plywood to fit windows as a last resort. Charge all your electronic devices – phones, tablets, power banks – while you still have electricity. Locate flashlights, extra batteries, and a battery-powered radio. Ensure your emergency kit is stocked with essentials like a first-aid kit, non-perishable food, and bottled water for at least three days per person.

Power Outage Plan

Power outages are common during severe weather. Know how to manually open your garage door if the power goes out. Keep a cooler ready with ice packs to preserve refrigerated food. If you have a generator, ensure it’s in good working order and that you know how to operate it safely (always outdoors, away from windows). Discuss a family meeting point outside your home in case of an emergency evacuation.

Communicate and Reassure

Before a storm hits, discuss your emergency plan with all family members. Designate an out-of-state contact person who everyone can call or text if local lines are down. This person can act as a central point of contact. Reassure children and anxious family members by explaining the preparations you’ve made. Being prepared not only keeps you safe but also brings peace of mind.
