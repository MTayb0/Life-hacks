Supreme Court Declines Appeal: Roundup Lawsuits to Continue Unabated

2026-06-25 18:30 UTC · 📰 News

The legal saga surrounding glyphosate-based weedkiller Roundup took a significant turn recently as the U.S. Supreme Court opted not to hear an appeal from Bayer, the German pharmaceutical and life sciences giant that acquired Monsanto, the original maker of Roundup. This decision, or rather non-decision, leaves in place lower court rulings that have favored plaintiffs claiming Roundup caused their non-Hodgkin lymphoma, opening the door for thousands more lawsuits to proceed.

A Decisive Non-Decision

Bayer had petitioned the Supreme Court multiple times to review cases where juries awarded large sums to plaintiffs, arguing that federal law on pesticide labeling should preempt state-level warnings. Specifically, the company contended that because the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) has found glyphosate not to be a carcinogen and does not require a cancer warning on its label, state-level lawsuits alleging a failure to warn should be dismissed. However, the High Court has consistently declined to intervene, effectively allowing the lower court verdicts and the associated legal theories to stand.

This steadfast refusal by the Supreme Court sends a clear message: Bayer's path to overturn these judgments via federal preemption is effectively closed. For the tens of thousands of plaintiffs still awaiting their day in court, this development is a substantial victory, reinforcing their ability to seek damages for illnesses they attribute to Roundup exposure.

The Core of the Controversy

At the heart of these lawsuits is the assertion that glyphosate, the active ingredient in Roundup, causes non-Hodgkin lymphoma, a type of cancer. While the EPA maintains that glyphosate is not likely to be carcinogenic to humans, the World Health Organization's International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classified it as "probably carcinogenic to humans" in 2015. This divergence in scientific opinion has fueled the contentious legal battles, with juries often siding with plaintiffs after hearing extensive expert testimony.

Bayer inherited these legal liabilities when it acquired Monsanto for $63 billion in 2018, and the ongoing litigation has weighed heavily on the company's financials and stock value. Despite setting aside billions for potential settlements, the Supreme Court's latest move means the fight will continue in trial courts, likely resulting in more significant payouts.

What This Means Going Forward

The Supreme Court's decision not to intervene marks a pivotal moment. It means Bayer will continue to face individual lawsuits and settlement negotiations, rather than having a broad legal shield based on federal regulatory approval. This outcome not only has profound implications for Bayer but also sets a precedent for product liability cases involving substances where scientific consensus is debated and regulatory stances differ from international bodies. For consumers, it underscores the persistent scrutiny over the safety of widely used products and the power of the judicial system to hold corporations accountable.
